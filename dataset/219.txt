       Hikyaku  From Wikipedia  the free encyclopedia    Jump to navigation Jump to search  Hikyaku from      woodblock print by    Katsushika Hokusai              Hikyaku      were couriers in Japan who carried letters  documents  bills of exchange  and packages  using a system of relay stations under the bakufu military governments  beginning in the Kamakura period              gradually yielding to more modern systems beginning in         Contents    Kamakura period   Edo period   Appearances in culture   References   Bibliography   Kamakura period edit   Courier or postman  Japan  hand coloured albumen print by Felice Beato  between      and      During the Asuka period the government established a system called Five Home Provinces and Seven Circuits  served by messengers known as ekishi      who carried special post station bells      ekirei    This system had broken down by the time of the Kamakura period  The Kamakura shogunate established a relay system  Roppara hikyaku  of horse riders and stages that would carry messages from the Rokuhara Tandai headquarters  in the Higashiyama area of Kyoto  to Kamakura  sometimes within    hours   Edo period edit  The hikyaku system reached a degree of sophistication that led to The Japanese Letter Writing Era  beginning about       There were many different types of hiyaku  including   Tsugi bikyaku        only available high ranking bakufu officials such as R j   elder statesmen   the Kyoto Shoshidai  Kyoto Deputy   the Osaka j dai  Governor of Osaka Castle   the governor of Sunpu Castle  Kanj  bugy   financial magistrates   and others of Bugy   magistrate  status  Daimyo bikyaku         couriers established by individual Daimyo  feudal lords  to carry messages between their domains and the domainal residence in Edo  and sometimes also to their rice warehouses in port cities  Kome bikyaku        couriers who carried news about rice prices from the D jima Rice Exchange in Osaka to interested parties elsewhere  Hikyaku tonya        or      hikyaku ya   commercial message carrying services available to everyone else  Tooshi bikyaku        a single runner who carried a message or parcel  without relay  from the sender to the addressee  Machi bikyaku        specialized runners within the Edo Bakufu  much used during the waning years of the Edo Bakufu  With bells jingling from their message boxes  they were called  chirin chirin no machi bikyaku  by the townsfolk  According to the Morisada Mango of        Their appearance was thus  the message box was painted in persimmon ink  the courier  place  and official s family name in vermilion ink  this box on a pole slung over the back  with wind chimes dangling from the front end of the pole  warning the crowds when the courier
